Chapter 1 Complex Variables

1.1 Complex numbers

Complex numbers are formed using the familiar real numbers and the imaginary unit i. The imaginary unit i is a special number with the property that

i2=−1,

Complex numbers can be written as

z=a+i⁢b.

Thus every complex number has a real part and an imaginary part,

Re⁡z=a,
Im⁡z=b.

Every real number can be considered a complex number whose imaginary part simply equals zero. The conjugate of z is denoted as z¯,

z¯=a−i⁢b.

Now we consider basic arithmetic operations on complex numbers.

Addition & subtraction

(a+i⁢b)+(c+i⁢d)=a+c+i⁢(b+d),
(a+i⁢b)−(c+i⁢d)=a−c+i⁢(b−d).

Multiplication

(a+i⁢b)⋅(c+i⁢d) =a⁢c+i⁢a⁢d+i⁢b⁢c−b⁢d
=(a⁢c−b⁢d)+i⁢(a⁢d+b⁢c).

Note that the fact i2=−1 is used in the above, and that (a+i⁢b)⋅(c+i⁢d)≠a⁢c+b⁢d⁢i!

Division

a+i⁢bc+i⁢d=a+i⁢bc+i⁢d⋅c−i⁢dc−i⁢d=(a⁢c+b⁢d)+i⁢(b⁢c−a⁢d)c2+d2.

If we consider the real part a of z as the x-coordinate, and the imaginary part b as the y-coordinate, then the complex number z can be identified with the point (a,b) on the x⁢y-plane. Thus it is customary to depict complex numbers as points on a 2D plane, which is then called a complex plane (Figure 1.1).

The modulus or absolute value of z is defined as the distance between z and the origin, and equals

|z|=a2+b2.

It is trivial to check that

|z|2=z⋅z¯.

The phase angle or argument of z is the angle between O⁢z and the positive x-axis. The phase angle θ satisfies the relation

tan⁡θ=ba,

from which one infers that

θ=arctan⁡baorθ=arctan⁡ba+π,

depending on which quadrant z is located. The phase angle is not unique, as θ±2⁢π⁢n, for any integer n, will also qualify as a phase angle for the same complex number. We note that the combination of modulus and phase angle completely determine the position of a complex number on the complex plane, and therefore the complex number itself. Indeed, a complex number can be expressed using its modulus and phase angle only,

z=|z|⁢(cos⁡θ+i⁢sin⁡θ).

The real and imaginary parts of z can also be explicitly given,

a=|z|⁢cos⁡θ
b=|z|⁢sin⁡θ.

It was discovered that the unit complex number cos⁡θ+i⁢sin⁡θ can be put in a more compact form,

ei⁢θ=cos⁡θ+i⁢sin⁡θ.

This identity was derived by Euler in 1740, and therefore it bears the name Euler Identity. It can be established using the Taylor series expansion of ei⁢θ, cos⁡θ and sin⁡θ around θ=0.

ei⁢θ =1+i⁢θ−θ22−i⁢θ33!+θ44!+i⁢θ55!−θ66!−i⁢θ77!+⋯
=(1−θ22+θ44!−θ66!+⋯)+i⁢(θ−θ33!+θ55!−θ77!−⋯)
=cos⁡θ+i⁢sin⁡θ.

Thus using the identity (2), a complex number can be succinctly expressed as

z=r⁢ei⁢θ,

with r=|z| standing for the modulus of z, θ=phase⁡(z) its phase angle. Expression (3) is often referred to as the polar form of complex number z. The polar form makes multiplication of complex numbers a lot easier. We note here that, for complex numbers z1=r1⁢ei⁢θ1, z2=r2⁢ei⁢θ2,

z1⋅z2=r1⁢ei⁢θ1⋅r2⁢ei⁢θ2=r1⁢r2⁢ei⁢(θ1+θ2),
zn=(r⁢ei⁢θ)n=rn⋅ei⁢n⁢θ(De Moivre’s Thm).

Example 1. Simplify the following complex number,

3−2⁢i−1+i=3−2⁢i−1+i⋅−1−i−1−i=−3−3⁢i+2⁢i−21+1=−5−i2.

Example 2. Express the complex number z=−6−i⁢2 in the polar form.

Solution: This complex number has modulus

r=6+2=2⁢2.

Its phase angle satisfies the relation

tan⁡θ=−2−6=13=33,

which means that it is either π/6, or 7⁢π/6. Considering that z is located in the third quadrant, we determine that

θ=76⁢π

is the correct answer. Thus,

z=2⁢2⁢ei⁢76⁢π.

We note that the polar form is not unique, for z can also be expressed as

z=2⁢2⁢ei⁢(2⁢n⁢π+76⁢π),for any integer ⁢n.

Example 3. Describe the curve given by the equation

|z−z0|=a.

Solution: It is a circle centered at z0, with radius a.
