3.2 Properties of Fourier transforms

1. Linearity

If f⁢(t) and g⁢(t) are functions with Fourier transforms f^⁢(ω) and g^⁢(ω), then the Fourier transform of c1⁢f⁢(t)+c2⁢g⁢(t) is a linear combination of f^⁢(ω) and g^⁢(ω), that is

ℱ⁢(c1⁢f⁢(t)+c2⁢g⁢(t))=c1⁢f^⁢(ω)+c2⁢g^⁢(ω),

where c1 and c2 are real or complex constants, and the operator ℱ stands for the Fourier transform.

2. Time shifting

Suppose function f⁢(t) has Fourier transform f^⁢(ω). Let τ be a non-zero parameter. Then the function f⁢(t−τ) is a shifting of the original function to the right by τ. Clearly, this new function f⁢(t−τ) is related to the original function f⁢(t). How are their Fourier transforms related? Knowing the Fourier transform of f⁢(t), is there a quick way to determine the Fourier transform of f⁢(t−τ)? The answer to this quesiton is provided by the time shifting property of Fourier transform.

We start from the definition of Fourier transform,

f⁢(t−τ)^ =∫−∞∞f⁢(t−τ)⁢e−i⁢ω⁢t⁢𝑑t
=∫−∞∞f⁢(s)⁢e−i⁢ω⁢(s+τ)⁢𝑑s(s=t−τ,d⁢s=d⁢t)
=e−i⁢ω⁢τ⁢∫−∞∞f⁢(s)⁢e−i⁢ω⁢s⁢𝑑s=e−i⁢ω⁢τ⁢f^⁢(ω),

Thus, we have

f⁢(t−τ)^=e−i⁢ω⁢τ⁢f^⁢(ω).

This formula shows that a shift of τ to the right in the physical space corresponds to a clockwise rotation of ω⁢τ in the frequency space.

3. Scaling

Let f⁢(t) be a function with a Fourier transform f^⁢(ω) and k be a real non-zero constant. Then the new function f⁢(k⁢t) is a re-scaling of the original function f⁢(t). Specifically, the graph of f⁢(k⁢t) can be obtained by compressing (if |k|>1) or stretching (|k|<1) the graph of f⁢(t), horizontally, by a factor of k. Clearly, f⁢(k⁢t) is related to the original function f⁢(t); how are their Fourier transform related? Can knowing the Fourier transform of f⁢(t) allow us to quickly obtain the Fourier transform of its scaled version, f⁢(k⁢t)?

We still start from the definition,

f⁢(k⁢t)^ =∫−∞∞f⁢(k⁢t)⁢e−i⁢ω⁢t⁢𝑑t
=∫−k⁢∞+k⁢∞f⁢(s)⁢e−i⁢ω⁢s/k⁢1k⁢𝑑s(s=k⁢t,d⁢s=k⁢d⁢t)
=1|k|⁢∫−∞∞f⁢(s)⁢e−i⁢(ω/k)⁢s⁢𝑑s
=1|k|⁢f^⁢(ωk),

In conclusion,

f⁢(k⁢t)^=f^⁢(ω/k)|k|.

Example 1

The Fourier transform of f⁢(t)=e−t⁢H⁢(t) is

f^⁢(ω)=11+i⁢ω.

Therefore the Fourier transform for f⁢(a⁢t)=e−a⁢t⁢H⁢(t), a>0, is

f⁢(a⁢t)^=1a⁢(11+i⁢ω/a)=1a+i⁢ω.

4. The symmetry rule

Suppose that function f⁢(t) has Fourier transform F⁢(ω). Viewing F⁢(t) now as a function of the physical variable t, what can we say about its Fourier transform? Back to the original f, as a function of the frequency variable ω? To find out, we start from an operation that connects these two functions, the inverse Fourier transform, given by formula (1) of the previous section,

f⁢(t)=12⁢π⁢∫−∞∞F⁢(ω)⁢ei⁢ω⁢t⁢𝑑ω.

This does look like Fourier transform itself, except for the sign in the exponent. To officially bring it into the form of Fourier transform, we set ω=s and t=−η. Then

f⁢(−η)=12⁢π⁢∫−∞∞F⁢(s)⁢e−i⁢η⁢s⁢𝑑s.

From this, we conclude that

F^⁢(η)=∫−∞∞F⁢(s)⁢e−i⁢η⁢s⁢𝑑s=2⁢π⁢f⁢(−η),

with η being the frequency variable. This derivation reveals to us that the Fourier transform of F is merely a symmetric reflection of f about the vertical axis. The artificial factor 2⁢π results from our choice of the factors in the specifications of the Fourier transform and its inverse.

Example 2

Find 1^, the Fourier transform of the constant function 1.

Solution: By the symmtry rule above, since

δ^⁢(ω)=1,

we have

1^=2⁢π⁢δ⁢(−ω)=2⁢π⁢δ⁢(ω).

The last equality above is due to the fact that the Dirac delta function is even. This is the first example where applying the definition of the Fourier transform will run into serious trouble. The symmetry rule, on the other hand, leads us to the final answer quickly.

Similarly, since (see Example 4 of last section)

δ⁢(t−a)^=e−i⁢ω⁢a,δ⁢(t+a)^=ei⁢ω⁢a,

by the symmetry rule,

ei⁢t⁢a^ =2⁢π⁢δ⁢(−ω+a)=2⁢π⁢δ⁢(ω−a),
e−i⁢t⁢a^ =2⁢π⁢δ⁢(−ω−a)=2⁢π⁢δ⁢(ω+a).

Example 3

Find the Fourier transform of 11+t2.

Solution: This is another example where applying the basic definition of Fourier transform only leads us to an integral that is very hard to evaluate. Fortunately, the symmetry rule provides an easy solution, relatively speaking. In Example 2 of last section, we saw that function e−a⁢|t| has Fourier transform 2⁢aa2+ω2. Thus, in this case,

f⁢(t)=e−a⁢|t|,and ⁢F⁢(ω)=2⁢aa2+ω2.

By the symmetry rule,

(2⁢aa2+t2)^=2⁢π⁢f⁢(−ω)=2⁢π⁢e−a⁢|−ω|=2⁢π⁢e−a⁢|ω|.

Taking a=1 in the above, we arrive at

(212+t2)^=2⁢π⁢e−|ω|.

Divide both sides by 2,

(112+t2)^=π⁢e−|ω|.

5. Differentiation

We assume that a function f⁢(t) has Fourier transform f^⁢(ω), and |f| is integrable, which implies, in particular, that

f⁢(−∞)=f⁢(∞)=0.

We also assume that f′⁢(t) exists. Then the Fourier transform of f′⁢(t) is

f′⁢(t)^ =∫−∞∞f′⁢(t)⁢e−i⁢ω⁢t⁢𝑑t
=∫−∞∞e−i⁢ω⁢t⁢𝑑f⁢(t)
=e−i⁢ω⁢t⁢f⁢(t)|−∞∞−∫−∞∞f⁢(t)⁢d⁢(e−i⁢ω⁢t)
=i⁢ω⁢∫−∞∞f⁢(t)⁢e−i⁢ω⁢t⁢𝑑t
=i⁢ω⁢f^⁢(ω).

Derivative in the physical space is translated to multiplication by a factor of i⁢ω in the frequency space.

By induction, and with appropriate assumptions on the higher order derivatives f(k)⁢(t), k=1,…,n, we derive that

f(k)⁢(t)^=(i⁢ω)k⁢f^⁢(ω),1≤k≤n.

Example 4

The Fourier transform of f⁢(t)=11+t2 is f^⁢(ω)=π⁢e−|ω|. Therefore

(−2⁢t(1+t2)2)^=(11+t2)′^=i⁢ω⁢π⁢e−|ω|.

Example 5: the heat equation

The heat equations govern the evolution of heat distribution in certain medium. In the one-dimensional case with uniform heat conductivity, the equations reads

{∂u∂t−κ⁢∂2u∂x2=0,u⁢(x,0)=u0⁢(x).

Here, u=u⁢(x,t) represents the heat distribution along a line, and the second equation provides the initial heat distribution at t=0.

There are several analytical and numerical methods for solving the heat equations. Here, we demonstrate how Fourier transform can be employed to find the solution to this system. We first apply Fourier transform with respect to x to the equation,

dd⁢t⁢u^−κ⁢(i⁢ω)2⁢u^=0⟹dd⁢t⁢u^=−κ⁢ω2⁢u^,

Note Fourier transform eliminates the spatial derivative via the differentiation rule. Fourier transform, again with respect to x, on the second equation yields

u^⁢(ω,0)=u^0⁢(ω).

Thus, under Fourier transform, the heat equation, which is a partial differential equation, becomes an ordinary differential equation, with an initial condition,

dd⁢t⁢u^=−κ⁢ω2⁢u^,
u^⁢(ω,0)=u^0⁢(ω).

We can easily write down the solution to this initial value problem,

u^⁢(ω,t)=u^0⁢(ω)⁢e−κ⁢ω2⁢t.

This solution is expressed in ω, the frequency variable. What is often wanted, in applications, is solution in the spatial variable. To get that, we can appy the inverse Fourier transform. This involves the inverse transform on a product of two functions, which we haven’t learned how to handle. Hence, we will postpone this last step, and instead we will present a few more properties.

6. Parseval’s equality

In physics and engineering, there is often a need to compute the energy of a system, and the energy is usually expressed by the integral of the square of a function f⁢(t). Can we still compute the energy if we only have the Fourier transform of the function? The answer is yes, and the result we are going to derive is called Parseval equality for non-periodic functions.

Let f⁢(t) be a real, non-periodic function. From the definition of inverse Fourier transform,

f⁢(t)=12⁢π⁢∫−∞∞f^⁢(ω)⁢ei⁢ω⁢t⁢𝑑ω.

We then derive that

∫−∞∞|f⁢(t)|2⁢𝑑t=∫−∞∞f⁢(t)∗⋅f⁢(t)⁢𝑑t
=12⁢π⁢∫−∞∞f⁢(t)∗⁢∫−∞∞f^⁢(ω)⁢ei⁢ω⁢t⁢𝑑ω⁢𝑑t

In above, the asterisk denotes the complex conjugate of a function. Exchanging the order of integrations, we continue,

∫−∞∞|f⁢(t)|2⁢𝑑t
=12⁢π⁢∫−∞∞f^⁢(ω)⁢∫−∞∞f⁢(t)∗⁢ei⁢ω⁢t⁢𝑑t⁢𝑑ω
=12⁢π⁢∫−∞∞f^⁢(ω)⁢(∫−∞∞f⁢(t)⁢e−i⁢ω⁢t⁢𝑑t)∗⁢𝑑ω
=12⁢π⁢∫−∞∞f^⁢(ω)⁢f^∗⁢(ω)⁢𝑑ω
=12⁢π⁢∫−∞∞|f^⁢(ω)|2⁢𝑑ω.

In the above, we have used the facts that e−i⁢ω⁢t=(ei⁢ω⁢t)∗, and (f⁢g)∗=f∗⁢g∗ for two arbitrary funcitons. To summarize,

∫−∞∞|f⁢(t)|2⁢𝑑t=12⁢π⁢∫−∞∞|f^⁢(ω)|2⁢𝑑ω.

The square integral of a function f⁢(t) over the real axis equals the square integral of its Fourier transform, up to an artificial factor of 1/2⁢π.

Example 6

Evaluate the integral

∫−∞∞(sin⁡xx)2⁢𝑑x.

Solution: This integral is very difficult to evaluate directly. We shall try to take a short cut, and use tools like the Parseval equality. In a previous example we showed that the Fourier transform of

f⁢(t)={1,−a<t<a,0,|t|>a,

is

f^⁢(ω)=2⁢sin⁡(ω⁢a)ω.

Hence, by the Parseval Equality for Fourier transforms,

∫−∞∞|f|2⁢𝑑t=12⁢π⁢∫−∞∞4⁢sin2⁡(ω⁢a)ω2⁢𝑑ω,
∫−aa1⁢𝑑t=2⁢a=12⁢π⁢∫−∞∞4⁢sin2⁡(ω⁢a)ω2⁢𝑑ω.

Therefore,

∫−∞∞sin2⁡(ω⁢a)ω2⁢𝑑ω=π⁢a.

Setting a=1, we arrive at

∫−∞∞sin2⁡(ω)ω2⁢𝑑ω=π.

7. Convolution

Let f⁢(t) and g⁢(t) be two functions. The convolution of f⁢(t) and g⁢(t) is another function, which is given by

f∗g⁢(t)=∫−∞∞f⁢(x)⁢g⁢(t−x)⁢𝑑x=∫−∞∞f⁢(t−x)⁢g⁢(x)⁢𝑑x.

The operation of convolution appears often in solving differential equations and in filter designs. Thus Fourier transform on convolutions is an important topic, and also an interesting one. We begin with the definition of the Fourier transform and convolution,

f∗g^⁢(ω) =∫−∞∞f∗g⁢(t)⁢e−i⁢ω⁢t⁢𝑑t
=∫−∞∞∫−∞∞f⁢(x)⁢g⁢(t−x)⁢𝑑x⁢e−i⁢ω⁢t⁢𝑑t
=∫−∞∞∫−∞∞f⁢(x)⁢g⁢(t−x)⁢e−i⁢ω⁢x⁢e−i⁢ω⁢(t−x)⁢𝑑x⁢𝑑t
=∫−∞∞f⁢(x)⁢e−i⁢ω⁢x⁢(∫−∞∞g⁢(t−x)⁢e−i⁢ω⁢(t−x)⁢𝑑t)⁢𝑑x
=∫−∞∞f⁢(x)⁢e−i⁢ω⁢x⁢𝑑x⁢∫−∞∞g⁢(s)⁢e−i⁢ω⁢s⁢𝑑s(s=t−x)
=f^⁢(ω)⁢g^⁢(ω).

Thus, the Fourier transform of the convolution of two functions equals the product of their respective Fourier transforms,

f∗g^⁢(ω)=f^⁢(ω)⁢g^⁢(ω).

Convolution in physical space corresponds to multiplication in frequency space!

The convolution operation can also be applied to Fourier transforms, in what is commonly referred to as frequency convolution. We now prove that

f⁢(t)⁢g⁢(t)^=12⁢π⁢f^⁢(ω)∗g^⁢(ω),

where

f^⁢(ω)∗g^⁢(ω)=∫−∞∞f^⁢(τ)⁢g^⁢(ω−τ)⁢𝑑τ.

We start with the inverse transform of f^⁢(ω):

f⁢(t)=12⁢π⁢∫−∞∞f^⁢(τ)⁢ei⁢τ⁢t⁢𝑑τ.

Multiply both sides by g⁢(t),

f⁢(t)⁢g⁢(t)=12⁢π⁢∫−∞∞f^⁢(τ)⁢ei⁢τ⁢t⁢g⁢(t)⁢𝑑τ.

Then

f⁢(t)⁢g⁢(t)^ =∫−∞∞12⁢π⁢∫−∞∞f^⁢(τ)⁢ei⁢τ⁢t⁢g⁢(t)⁢𝑑τ⁢e−i⁢ω⁢t⁢𝑑t
=12⁢π⁢∫−∞∞f^⁢(τ)⁢∫−∞∞g⁢(t)⁢e−i⁢(ω−τ)⁢t⁢𝑑t⁢𝑑τ
=12⁢π⁢∫−∞∞f^⁢(τ)⁢g^⁢(ω−τ)⁢𝑑τ
=12⁢π⁢f^⁢(ω)∗g^⁢(ω).

Thus, the Fourier transform of the product of two functions, as it turns out, equals the convolution of the their respective Fourier transforms,

f⁢(t)⁢g⁢(t)^=12⁢π⁢f^⁢(ω)∗g^⁢(ω).

Multiplication in the physical space corresponds to convolution in the frequency space!

  For the remainder of this section, we want to explore the relation between Fourier transform with Fourier series, and we will show that they are actually the same thing, manifest differently on different functions. We accomplish this mission by doing two examples.

Example 6

Find Fourier transforms for sin⁡a⁢t and cos⁡a⁢t.

Solution: We note that

cos⁡a⁢t=12⁢[ei⁢a⁢t+e−i⁢a⁢t].

Using results from Example 2 in the above,

cos⁡a⁢t^=12⁢[ei⁢a⁢t^+e−i⁢a⁢t^]=π⁢[δ⁢(ω−a)+δ⁢(ω+a)],

which represents two pulses at ω=a and ω=−a, each with weight π.

Similarly, for sin⁡a⁢t,

sin⁡(a⁢t)=12⁢i⁢(ei⁢a⁢t−e−i⁢a⁢t),
sin⁡(a⁢t)^=12⁢i⁢(ei⁢a⁢t^−e−i⁢a⁢t^)=−i⁢π⁢[δ⁢(ω−a)−δ⁢(ω+a)].

Again, this represents two pulses at ω=a and ω=−a, with weight −i⁢π and i⁢π, respectively.

Example 7

Suppose f⁢(t) is a periodic function with period 2⁢L. Find its Fourier transform.

Solution:
We write its Fourier series (complex form) as

f⁢(t)=∑n=−∞∞cn⁢ei⁢n⁢πL⁢t=∑n=−∞∞cn⁢ei⁢ωn⁢t,with ⁢ωn=n⁢πL.

Then, taking Fourier transform on both sides and using conclusions from the last example, we have

f^⁢(ω) =∑n=−∞∞cn⁢ei⁢ωn⁢t^
=∑n=−∞∞2⁢π⁢cn⁢δ⁢(ω−ωn).

Thus, the Fourier transform of an arbitrary periodic function is a sequence of impulses with weights 2⁢π⁢cn at ω=ωn. Those weights are the coefficients in the complex Fourier series expansion of the function!
