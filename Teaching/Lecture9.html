4.2 Orthogonality of eigenfunctions

In this section, we turn to the orthogonality between the eigenfunctions of a given Sturm-Liouville problems. In a Euclidean space, two vectors 𝒗 and 𝒘 are said to be orthogonal when

𝒗⋅𝒘=0,∙ denotes inner product between vectors.

Viewing functions as “vector”’s in a certain function space, we can define their inner products, and the condition for the orthogonality between them.

Orthogonality of eigenfunctions is a fundamental property, just as the orthogonality of the unit base vectors of a Cartesian coordinate system. With orthogonality, eigenfunctions of a given Sturm-Liouville problem can also be used as a basis, on which, other functions can be expanded, just as they can be expanded as a Fourier series of sine and cosine functions.

For convenience, we repeat the general form of the Sturm-Liouville problems of the last section,

dd⁢x⁢[p⁢(x)⁢d⁢yd⁢x]+[q⁢(x)+λ⁢r⁢(x)]⁢y=0,a≤x≤b,
α⁢y⁢(a)+β⁢y′⁢(a)=0,
γ⁢y⁢(b)+δ⁢y′⁢(b)=0.

Regarding the orthogonality of the eigenfucntions of Sturm-Liouville problems of this form, we have

Theorem 3.

Let the coefficient functions p⁢(x)>0, q⁢(x) and r⁢(x)>0 of the regular Sturm–Liouville problem above be real and continuous on the interval [a,b]. If yn⁢(x) and ym⁢(x) are continuously differentiable eigenfunctions corresponding to distinct eigenvalues λn and λm, respectively, then yn⁢(x) and ym⁢(x) satisfy the orthogonality condition

∫abr⁢(x)⁢yn⁢(x)⁢ym⁢(x)⁢𝑑x=0. (1)

When Eq. (1) is satisfied, the eigenfunctions yn⁢(x) and ym⁢(x) are said to be orthogonal to each other with respect to the weight function r⁢(x).

Proof: Since yn⁢(x) and ym⁢(x) are eigenfunctions associated with eigenvalues λn and λm respectively, the following relations hold:

dd⁢x⁢(p⁢(x)⁢yn′⁢(x))+(q⁢(x)+λn⁢r⁢(x))⁢yn⁢(x)=0, (2)
dd⁢x⁢(p⁢(x)⁢ym′⁢(x))+(q⁢(x)+λm⁢r⁢(x))⁢ym⁢(x)=0. (3)

Multiplying (2) by ym, and Eq. (3) by yn, and subtracting, we have

dd⁢x⁢(p⁢(x)⁢yn′⁢(x))⁢ym−dd⁢x⁢(p⁢(x)⁢ym′⁢(x))⁢yn+λn⁢r⁢(x)⁢ym⁢(x)⁢yn⁢(x)−λm⁢r⁢(x)⁢ym⁢(x)⁢yn⁢(x)=0.

After some rearrangements, we arrive at

(λm−λn)⁢r⁢(x)⁢ym⁢(x)⁢yn⁢(x)=(p⁢(x)⁢yn′⁢(x))′⁢ym⁢(x)−(p⁢(x)⁢ym′⁢(x))′⁢yn⁢(x). (4)

Integrating Eq. (4) from a to b yields

(λm−λn)⁢∫abr⁢(x)⁢ym⁢(x)⁢yn⁢(x)⁢𝑑x=∫ab[dd⁢x⁢(p⁢yn′)⁢ym−dd⁢x⁢(p⁢ym′)⁢yn]⁢𝑑x. (5)

In order to establishing the orthogonality between the eigenfunctions, it is clear that we need to show the right-hand side of equation (5) vanishes. We proceed with integration by parts,

∫ab[dd⁢x⁢(p⁢yn′)⁢ym−dd⁢x⁢(p⁢ym′)⁢yn]⁢𝑑x
=p⁢yn′⁢ym|ab−∫abp⁢yn′⁢ym′⁢𝑑x−p⁢ym′⁢yn|ab+∫abp⁢ym′⁢yn′⁢𝑑x
=(p⁢yn′⁢ym−p⁢ym′⁢yn)|ab
=p⁢(b)⁢yn′⁢(b)⁢ym⁢(b)−p⁢(b)⁢ym′⁢(b)⁢yn⁢(b)−p⁢(a)⁢yn′⁢(a)⁢ym⁢(a)+p⁢(a)⁢ym′⁢(a)⁢yn⁢(a)
=p⁢(b)⁢[yn′⁢(b)⁢ym⁢(b)−ym′⁢(b)⁢yn⁢(b)]−p⁢(a)⁢[yn′⁢(a)⁢ym⁢(a)−ym′⁢(a)⁢yn⁢(a)].

Let’s focus on the point x=b first. Here both ym,yn satisfy the BC’s

γ⁢ym⁢(b)+δ⁢ym′⁢(b)=0,
γ⁢yn⁢(b)+δ⁢yn′⁢(b)=0.

This can be viewed as a linear system of γ and δ. Since γ and δ are not identically zero, the coefficient matrix

(ym⁢(b)ym′⁢(b)yn⁢(b)yn′⁢(b))

must be singular, i.e. its determinant vanishes,

ym⁢(b)⁢yn′⁢(b)−ym′⁢(b)⁢yn⁢(b)=0.

Similarly, we can argue that

ym⁢(a)⁢yn′⁢(a)−ym′⁢(a)⁢yn⁢(a)=0.

Therefore, the right-hand side of Eq. (5) indeed vanishes, and we do have

∫abr⁢(x)⁢ym⁢(x)⁢yn⁢(x)⁢𝑑x=0,provided ⁢λm≠λn.

This completes the proof that the eigenfunctions associated with distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal with respect to the weight function r⁢(x).

Example 1

The Sturm–Liouville problem

{y′′+λ⁢y=0,y⁢(0)=y⁢(π)=0

has

eigenvalues:λk=k2,k≥1,eigenfunctions:yk=sin⁡(k⁢x).

In this case, r⁢(x)≡1. It can be easily verified that

∫0πsin⁡(m⁢x)⁢sin⁡(n⁢x)⁢𝑑x=0if ⁢m≠n.

Recall that sine functions are the primary elements of the half-range expansion of odd funtions in Chapter 2. There, the orthogonality property between these functions played a decisive role in the calculations of the coefficients.

Example 2

The Sturm–Liouville problem

{y′′+λ⁢y=0,0<x<π,y⁢(0)=0,y⁢(π)+y′⁢(π)=0

has, for k≥1,

eigenvalues:λk=ξk2,ξk>0⁢ the ⁢kth⁢ root of ⁢ξ=−tan⁡(ξ⁢π),eigenfunctions:yk=sin⁡(ξk⁢x).

We now verify via direct calculations that ym and yn are orthogonal, provided m≠n.

∫0πsin⁡(ξm⁢x)⁢sin⁡(ξn⁢x)⁢𝑑x =∫0π12⁢(cos⁡((ξm−ξn)⁢x)−cos⁡((ξm+ξn)⁢x))⁢𝑑x
=12⁢[sin⁡((ξm−ξn)⁢x)ξm−ξn|0π−sin⁡((ξm+ξn)⁢x)ξm+ξn|0π]
=12⁢[sin⁡((ξm−ξn)⁢π)ξm−ξn−sin⁡((ξm+ξn)⁢π)ξm+ξn]
=12⁢(ξm−ξn)⁢(ξm+ξn)⁢[(ξm+ξn)⁢sin⁡((ξm−ξn)⁢π)−(ξm−ξn)⁢sin⁡((ξm+ξn)⁢π)].

Using the sine sum and difference formulas,

12⁢(ξm−ξn)⁢(ξm+ξn)[(ξm+ξn)(sinξmπcosξnπ−cosξmπsinξnπ)
−(ξm−ξn)(sinξmπcosξnπ+cosξmπsinξnπ)]
=12⁢(ξm−ξn)⁢(ξm+ξn)⁢[2⁢ξn⁢sin⁡ξm⁢π⁢cos⁡ξn⁢π−2⁢ξm⁢cos⁡ξm⁢π⁢sin⁡ξn⁢π].

We note that

ξm=−tan⁡(ξm⁢π),ξn=−tan⁡(ξn⁢π).

Hence

ξn⁢sin⁡ξm⁢π⁢cos⁡ξn⁢π−ξm⁢cos⁡ξm⁢π⁢sin⁡ξn⁢π=−sin⁡ξm⁢π⁢sin⁡ξn⁢π+sin⁡ξm⁢π⁢sin⁡ξn⁢π=0.

Thus

∫0πym⁢(x)⁢yn⁢(x)⁢𝑑x=∫0πsin⁡(ξm⁢x)⁢sin⁡(ξn⁢x)⁢𝑑x=0,provided ⁢m≠n.

Expansions in series of eigenfunctions

Sturm–Liouville problems often appear when we use separation of variables method to solve partial differential equations. There, the only way to explicitly express the solutions of the PDEs is to write them as series involving the eigenfunctions of the associated Sturm–Liouville problems. In this section we explore the expansion of functions in terms of the eigenfunctions of regular Sturm–Liouville problems.

Let yn⁢(x), n≥0, be eigenfunctions of a regular Sturm–Liouville problem

{dd⁢x⁢(p⁢(x)⁢y′⁢(x))+(q⁢(x)+λ⁢r⁢(x))⁢y=0,a<x<b,BC’s.

Let f⁢(x) be defined in the interval a<x<b. Under most circumstances, the function can be expressed as a series of yn⁢(x), that is, we can write

f⁢(x)=∑n=0∞cn⁢yn⁢(x). (6)

The orthogonality relations, introduced in the above, can be employed to compute the coefficients. For a certain m≥0, we multiply (2) by r⁢(x)⁢ym⁢(x), and integrate over (a,b),

∫abr⁢(x)⁢f⁢(x)⁢ym⁢(x)⁢𝑑x=∑n=0∞cn⁢∫abr⁢(x)⁢yn⁢(x)⁢ym⁢(x)⁢𝑑x. (7)

We note that

∫abr⁢(x)⁢yn⁢(x)⁢ym⁢(x)⁢𝑑x=0if ⁢n≠m.

Hence the right-hand side of (7) collapses into one single term,

∫abr⁢(x)⁢f⁢(x)⁢ym⁢(x)⁢𝑑x=cm⁢∫abr⁢(x)⁢ym2⁢(x)⁢𝑑x,

from which we derive a formula for the coefficient cm,

cm=∫abr⁢(x)⁢f⁢(x)⁢ym⁢(x)⁢𝑑x∫abr⁢(x)⁢ym2⁢(x)⁢𝑑x.

Example 3

Find the expansion of f⁢(x)=x over the interval [0,π], using the eigenfunctions of the Sturm–Liouville problem

{y′′+λ⁢y=0,y⁢(0)=0,y⁢(π)+y′⁢(π)=0. (9)

Solution: Recall that the Sturm–Liouville problem has eigenvalues λn=ξn2, ξn>0 being nth root of ξ=−tan⁡(ξ⁢π), and eigenfunctions

yn⁢(x)=sin⁡(ξn⁢x).

These eigenfunctions provide a basis, on which the function f⁢(x)=x can be expanded,

x=∑n=1∞cn⁢sin⁡(ξn⁢x).

With r⁢(x)≡1 in this case, and by (8), the coefficient cn is given by

cn=∫0πx⁢sin⁡(ξn⁢x)⁢𝑑x∫0πsin2⁡(ξn⁢x)⁢𝑑x.

We note that

∫0πx⁢sin⁡(ξn⁢x)⁢𝑑x =∫0πx⁢d⁢(cos⁡(ξn⁢x)−ξn)
=x⁢cos⁡(ξn⁢x)−ξn|0π+∫0πcos⁡(ξn⁢x)ξn⁢𝑑x
=π⁢cos⁡(π⁢ξn)−ξn+sin⁡(ξn⁢x)ξn2|0π
=sin⁡(ξn⁢π)−π⁢ξn⁢cos⁡(ξn⁢π)ξn2.

Also

∫0πsin2⁡(ξn⁢x)⁢𝑑x =∫0π12⁢(1−cos⁡(2⁢ξn⁢x))⁢𝑑x
=12⁢x−sin⁡(2⁢ξn⁢x)4⁢ξn|0π
=π2−sin⁡(2⁢ξn⁢π)4⁢ξn.

Hence

cn =sin⁡(ξn⁢π)−π⁢ξn⁢cos⁡(ξn⁢π)ξn2π2−sin⁡(2⁢ξn⁢π)4⁢ξn
=sin⁡(ξn⁢π)−ξn⁢π⁢cos⁡(ξn⁢π)π2⁢ξn2−ξn4⁢sin⁡(2⁢ξn⁢π).

This expression is already acceptable. For further simplification, we note that

sin⁡(ξn⁢π)=−ξn⁢cos⁡(ξn⁢π),
sin⁡(2⁢ξn⁢π)=2⁢sin⁡(ξn⁢π)⁢cos⁡(ξn⁢π)=−2⁢ξn⁢cos2⁡(ξn⁢π).

Hence, we have

cn =−ξn⁢cos⁡(ξn⁢π)−ξn⁢π⁢cos⁡(ξn⁢π)π2⁢ξn2+ξn24⁢ 2⁢cos2⁡(ξn⁢π)
=−ξn⁢(1+π)⁢cos⁡(ξn⁢π)ξn22⁢(π+cos2⁡(ξn⁢π))
=−2⁢(1+π)⁢cos⁡(ξn⁢π)ξn⁢(π+cos2⁡(ξn⁢π)).

 

We conclude this section with a theorem, which provides a theoretical foundation for what we did in the above, namely it guarantees that the eigenfunctions of a regular Sturm-Liouville problem form a proper basis on which a general function, subject to certain regularity assumptions, can be expanded, and the expansion converges to the underlying function in the Dirichlet’s sense, just like the Fourier series (c.f. Theorem 1 of Chapter 2).

Theorem 4.

If both f⁢(x) and f′⁢(x) are piecewise continuous in [a,b], then f⁢(x) can be expanded in a uniformly convergent Fourier series involving eigenfunctions of a regular Sturm–Liouville problem on the same interval. The series converges to [f⁢(x+)+f⁢(x−)]/2 at any point x in the open interval a<x<b.

4.3 A singular Sturm–Liouville problem: Legendre’s equation

So far we have only considered regular Sturm–Liouville problems, where both p⁢(x) and r⁢(x) are strictly positive. But singular Sturm–Liouville problems do arise in practice. It is important to know whether these singular Sturm-Liouville problems have any eigenvalues/eigenfunctions, and whether these eigenfunctions form a base for series expansion of other functions. There is not a general strategy that can treat all singular Sturm–Liouville problems. Instead, in this section, we will consider a special case, the Legendre’s equation.

We start with the general form of Sturm–Liouville:

dd⁢x⁢(p⁢(x)⁢y′⁢(x))+(q⁢(x)+λ⁢r⁢(x))⁢y⁢(x)=0,a≤x≤b. (1)

Here, p⁢(x) & r⁢(x) may be zero or negative, and no BC’s are specified. As we have seen in last section, othogonality is a crucial property for the eigenfunctions of a Sturm-Liouville problem; it is what makes them useful in applications. Here, we would like to know what conditions are needed in order to preserve the orthogonality of the eigenfunctions, even when the Sturm–Liouville problem itself may be singular. Previously, we showed that, if (λm,ym) and (λn,yn) are two eigen-pairs to (1), then we have

(λn−λm)⁢∫abr⁢(x)⁢yn⁢(x)⁢ym⁢(x)⁢𝑑x=[p⁢(b)⁢ym′⁢(b)⁢yn⁢(b)−p⁢(b)⁢yn′⁢(b)⁢ym⁢(b)]−[p⁢(a)⁢ym′⁢(a)⁢yn⁢(a)−p⁢(a)⁢yn′⁢(a)⁢ym⁢(a)]. (4.1)

We observe that, as long as

y⁢(x)⁢ remains finite at ⁢x=a,b, (3)
p⁢(x)⁢y′⁢(x)=0at ⁢x=a,b, (4)

then the right-hand side of (2) vanishes, and the orthogonality is preserved. We will use (3)+(4) as criteria to accept or reject possible solutions to the singular Sturm–Liouville problem.

Let’s now consider the Legendre’s equation,

dd⁢x⁢[(1−x2)⁢y′⁢(x)]+n⁢(n+1)⁢y⁢(x)=0,−1≤x≤1, (5a)

or, equivalently,

(1−x2)⁢d2⁢yd⁢x2−2⁢x⁢y′⁢(x)+n⁢(n+1)⁢y⁢(x)=0. (5b)

Because p⁢(x)≡1−x2=0 at x=±1, this is a singular Sturm–Liouville problem. A general form for the solutions of (5) is not known. The polynomial coefficient suggests that a power series may be used to solve (5). We assume that the solution takes the form

y⁢(x)=∑k=0∞Ak⁢xk. (6)

We proceed with term-by-term differentiation,

y′⁢(x)=∑k=0∞k⁢Ak⁢xk−1, (7)
y′′⁢(x)=∑k=0∞k⁢(k−1)⁢Ak⁢xk−2. (8)

Substituting (6), (7) & (8) into (5b) yields

(1−x2)⁢∑k=0∞k⁢(k−1)⁢Ak⁢xk−2−2⁢x⁢∑k=0∞k⁢Ak⁢xk−1+n⁢(n+1)⁢∑k=0∞Ak⁢xk=0,
∑k=0∞k⁢(k−1)⁢Ak⁢xk−2−∑k=0∞k⁢(k−1)⁢Ak⁢xk−2⁢∑k=0∞k⁢Ak⁢xk+n⁢(n+1)⁢∑k=0∞Ak⁢xk=0,
∑k=2∞k⁢(k−1)⁢Ak⁢xk−2+∑k=0∞(n⁢(n+1)−k⁢(k−1)−2⁢k)⁢Ak⁢xk=0,

In the first summation, we make the change of variable m=k−2 so that the new index starts from zero as well,

∑m=0∞(m+2)⁢(m+1)⁢Am+2⁢xm+∑k=0∞(n⁢(n+1)−k⁢(k−1)−2⁢k)⁢Ak⁢xk=0.

Now, we switch m back to k to combine the two summations,

∑k=0∞(k+2)⁢(k+1)⁢Ak+2⁢xk+∑k=0∞(n⁢(n+1)−k⁢(k−1)−2⁢k)⁢Ak⁢xk=0,
∑k=0∞[(k+2)⁢(k+1)⁢Ak+2+(n⁢(n+1)−k⁢(k+1))⁢Ak]⁢xk=0.

For this series to vanish for arbitrary x∈[−1,1], the coefficients must vanish identically. Hence

(k+2)⁢(k+1)⁢Ak+2=[k⁢(k+1)−n⁢(n+1)]⁢Ak, (9)

or

Ak+2=k⁢(k+1)−n⁢(n+1)(k+2)⁢(k+1)⁢Ak=(−1)⁢(n+k+1)⁢(n−k)(k+2)⁢(k+1)⁢Ak. (10)

This is a recursive relation, and it needs A0 and A1 in order to generate all the subsequent coefficients Ak, k≥2.

If A0=1, relation (10) generates a sequence

A0=1,
A2=−n⁢(n+1)2,
A4=3⋅2−n⁢(n+1)4⋅3⁢A2=3⋅2−n⁢(n+1)4⋅3⋅−n⁢(n+1)2=(n−2)⁢n⁢(n+1)⁢(n+3)4!,
A6=5⋅4−n⁢(n+1)6⋅5⁢A4=−(n+5)⁢(n−4)6⋅5⋅(n−2)⁢n⁢(n+1)⁢(n+3)4!=−(n−4)⁢(n−2)⁢n⁢(n+1)⁢(n+3)⁢(n+5)6!,
A2⁢k=(−1)k⁢(n−2⁢k+2)⁢(n−4)⁢⋯⁢(n−2)⁢n⁢(n+1)⁢(n+3)⁢⋯⁢(n+2⁢k−1)(2⁢k)!.

If A1=1, then relation (10) produces the following sequence

A1=1,
A3=−(n−1)⁢(n+2)3×2,
A5=(−1)⁢(n−3)⁢(n+4)5×4⁢A3=(n−3)⁢(n−1)⁢(n+2)⁢(n+4)5×4×3×2,
A2⁢k+1=(−1)k⁢(n−2⁢k+1)⁢(n−2⁢k+3)⁢⋯⁢(n−1)⁢(n+2)⁢(n+4)⁢⋯⁢(n+2⁢k)(2⁢k+1)!.

We observe that if n is an even positive integer, then the sequence A0,A2,A4,… will terminate (i.e. becomes zero) for k≥(n+2)/2. In this case, we get a polynomial of finite order if we take A0=1 but A1=0. Similarly, if n is an odd positive integer, then the sequence A1,A3,A5,… will terminate (i.e. becomes zero) for k≥(n+1)/2. In this case, with the choices A0=0, A1=1, we obtain a polynomial of finite order as the solution of (5).

Finite-order polynomials that are solutions of the Legendre’s equation are called Legendre’s functions of the first kind, and denoted by Pn⁢(x). Infinite-order polynomials that are solutions of the Legendre’s equation are called Legendre’s functions of the second kind, and are denoted by Qn⁢(x). We are interested in finite-order polynomials, because they satisfy the conditions (3)+(4) for orthogonality; that is, Legendre’s functions of the first kind are orthogonal to each other.

We have just derived polynomials of finite order as solutons to the Legendre’s equation. The forms in which they are given are not very convenient to use, unfortunately. We need a form that allows us to quickly compute the polynomial of an arbitrary order. Below, we introduce two new forms for Legendre’s polynomials.

Rodrigue’s formula for Pn⁢(x)

Pn⁢(x)=12n⁢n!⁢dnd⁢xn⁢(x2−1)n. (11)

By (11),

P0⁢(x)=1,
P1⁢(x)=12⁢dd⁢x⁢(x2−1)=x,
P2⁢(x) =14×2⁢d2d⁢x2⁢(x2−1)2=18⁢d2d⁢x2⁢(x4−2⁢x2+1)
=18⁢dd⁢x⁢(4⁢x3−4⁢x)=18⁢(12⁢x2−4)=32⁢x2−12.

The calculations get long and tedious as n goes up, and so this formula is not very useful either for large n’s.

A recurrence formula

Legendre’s polynomials of consecutive orders (n−1, n, and n+1) satisfy the relation

(n+1)⁢Pn+1⁢(x)=(2⁢n+1)⁢x⁢Pn⁢(x)−n⁢Pn−1⁢(x),for ⁢n≥1. (12)

Knowing that P0⁢(x)=1, P1⁢(x)=x, we use (12) to calculate P2⁢(x) by setting n=1 in (12),

2⁢P2⁢(x)=3⁢x⁢P1⁢(x)−P0⁢(x)=3⁢x2−1,
P2⁢(x)=32⁢x2−12.

With P1 and P2 now at hand, we can use (12) again to find P3,

3⁢P3⁢(x) =5⁢x⁢P2⁢(x)−2⁢P1⁢(x)
=5⁢x⁢(32⁢x2−12)−2⁢x
=152⁢x3−92⁢x,
P3⁢(x)=52⁢x3−32⁢x.

The process can be repeated to find Legendre’s polynomials of arbitrary orders.

Orthogonal relation

Legendre’s polynomials are orthogonal by design, with respect to the weight function r(x)≡=1. Specifically, we have the following integral relation,

∫−11Pn⁢(x)⁢Pm⁢(x)⁢𝑑x={0,m≠n,22⁢n+1,m=n. (13)

Series expansion in terms of Legendre’s polynomials

Let f⁢(x) be an arbitrary piecewise differentiable function in (−1,1). We write f⁢(x) as a series in terms of Pn⁢(x),

f⁢(x)=∑n=0∞an⁢Pn⁢(x). (14)

To determine the coefficients an, we multiply (14) by Pm⁢(x), and integrate over (−1,1),

∫−11f⁢(x)⁢Pm⁢(x)⁢𝑑x=∑n=0∞an⁢∫−11Pm⁢(x)⁢Pn⁢(x)⁢𝑑x.

Using the orthogonal relation,

∫−11f⁢(x)⁢Pn⁢(x)⁢𝑑x=an⁢∫−11Pn2⁢(x)⁢𝑑x=an⁢22⁢n+1.

Thus

an=2⁢n+12⁢∫−11f⁢(x)⁢Pn⁢(x)⁢𝑑x. (15)

If f⁢(x) and its first n derivatives are continuous on (−1,1), then an can be evaluated by using Rodrigue’s formula for Pn⁢(x),

an =2⁢n+12⁢12n⁢n!⁢∫−11f⁢(x)⁢dn⁢(x2−1)nd⁢xn⁢𝑑x
=2⁢n+12⁢12n⁢n!⁢(−1)n⁢∫−11f(n)⁢(x)⁢(x2−1)n⁢𝑑x.

Hence

an=2⁢n+12n+1⁢n!⁢∫−11f(n)⁢(x)⁢(1−x2)n⁢𝑑x. (16)

A particularly useful result follows from (16). Notice that if f⁢(x) is itself a polynomial of degree k, then an=0 if n>k. Consequently, a polynomial of degree k can be expressed as a linear combination of the first k+1 Legendre’s polynomials

[P0⁢(x),P1⁢(x),…,Pk⁢(x)].

Example 1

Express f⁢(x)=x2 in terms of Legendre’s polynomials.

Solution: According to the discussion above, we only need to consider Legendre’s polynomials up to degree 2.

x2=A0⁢P0+A1⁢P1+A2⁢P2,
=A0+A1⁢x+A2⁢(32⁢x2−12).
